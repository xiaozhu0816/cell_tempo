\section*{SUMMARY}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Automated detection of viral infection from label-free microscopy is critical for high-throughput antiviral screening, yet single-frame methods fail during late-stage infection when damaged cell monolayers resemble dense uninfected cultures.
Here, we present a temporal pseudo-RGB encoding strategy combined with a unified four-class multi-task model for robust infection detection in human brain microvascular endothelial cells (HBMVEC) infected with Venezuelan equine encephalitis virus (VEEV) TC-83.
Three time-offset brightfield frames ($t{-}6$h, $t{-}3$h, $t$) are stacked as pseudo-RGB channels, enabling standard pretrained CNNs to learn morphological trajectories.
Using cross-well validation across three biological replicates, our unified temporal four-class multi-task model achieves 93.5\% derived binary detection accuracy, 70.6\% four-class severity accuracy, and a time-regression MAE of 1.34 hours (R$^2 = 0.981$), while maintaining a $+$22 percentage-point binary advantage at 40--48 hours where single-frame methods collapse.
This unified formulation keeps binary infected-versus-mock detection as the primary readout while simultaneously providing severity classification and continuous time-since-infection estimation from the same forward pass.

\section*{INTRODUCTION}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Viral infections remain a persistent global health burden, with emerging and re-emerging pathogens posing significant threats to public health.\cite{ref-morens2004emerging,ref-woolhouse2005emerging}
Cytopathic effects (CPE)---morphological changes including cell rounding, shrinkage, and detachment---serve as hallmark indicators of productive viral infection (Figure~1).\cite{ref-hsiung1994cpe,ref-leland2007cpe}
However, CPE development varies in timing and severity depending on the virus, host cell type, and multiplicity of infection (MOI), making standardized quantification challenging.\cite{ref-zandi2019cpe}
A particularly underappreciated difficulty is that morphological changes during early infection are often exceedingly subtle, while at late stages, extensively damaged monolayers may paradoxically resemble dense uninfected cultures, confounding both manual scoring and automated analysis.\cite{ref-wang2020cpe,ref-petkidis2023review}

Human brain microvascular endothelial cells (HBMVEC) constitute a critical component of the blood--brain barrier (BBB).\cite{ref-daneman2015bbb,ref-abbott2010bbb}
Venezuelan equine encephalitis virus (VEEV), an alphavirus classified as a Category B select agent, can traverse the BBB through infection of brain endothelial cells.\cite{ref-weaver2004veev,ref-schafer2011vee,ref-salimi2020mbio}
HBMVEC infected with VEEV thus provide a clinically relevant model for studying neurotropic viral infection dynamics.

Label-free brightfield microscopy offers distinct advantages for infection monitoring: it is non-invasive, compatible with continuous longitudinal imaging, and avoids artifacts from exogenous labels or destructive endpoint assays.\cite{ref-riss2016assay,ref-petkidis2023review}
Recent deep learning approaches have demonstrated promising results for automated CPE detection from brightfield images.\cite{ref-andriasyan2021iscience,ref-petkidis2024natcomm,ref-chen2022biomedicines}
However, these systems treat each frame independently, discarding the temporal trajectory information inherent in time-lapse data.

The central challenge is that single-frame classification is inherently brittle: at late time points (40--48 hours post-infection), extensively damaged infected monolayers and dense confluent mock cultures share similar static features.
We hypothesize that encoding the \emph{trajectory} of morphological change---not just the current snapshot---can resolve this ambiguity throughout the full infection timeline.

To address this, we propose temporal pseudo-RGB encoding: three time-offset frames ($t{-}6$h, $t{-}3$h, $t$) are stacked as red, green, and blue channels, creating a pseudo-color image where morphological change appears as color variation (Figure~2).
Combined with a unified four-class multi-task framework, this representation enables binary infection detection to remain the primary readout while jointly learning severity grading and time-post-infection regression throughout 48 hours of VEEV infection in HBMVEC.

Our approach makes several contributions.
First, we demonstrate robust binary infection detection from label-free brightfield microscopy using a unified temporal four-class multi-task model, achieving 93.5\% derived binary accuracy and maintaining reliable performance at 40--48 hours where single-frame methods collapse ($+$22 pp advantage).
Second, we introduce temporal pseudo-RGB encoding, an architecture-agnostic method that encodes morphological trajectory by stacking time-offset frames as pseudo-RGB channels.
Third, the same unified model simultaneously supports four-class severity classification (70.6\% accuracy across four MOI levels) and time-post-infection regression (MAE $= 1.34$h, R$^2 = 0.981$).
Fourth, we validate generalization using a stringent cross-well row-split protocol across three biological replicates and held-out wells.

\section*{RESULTS}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

\subsection*{Component ablation establishes the contribution of each design element}

To quantify the contribution of temporal encoding and multi-task learning while keeping binary infected-versus-mock detection as the primary evaluation target, we performed a systematic component ablation across binary, four-class, and regression settings (Table~1).
This comparison clarifies how the final unified temporal four-class multi-task model relates to simpler single-task and single-frame baselines.

The baseline single-frame binary classifier achieved 89.2\% binary accuracy.
Adding temporal encoding alone improved binary detection to 92.1\%, establishing temporal encoding as the primary driver of classification improvement.
Adding multi-task regression to the single-frame model yielded 90.7\% binary accuracy (+1.5 pp), showing that the auxiliary time-regression signal provides complementary supervisory information even without temporal encoding.
The dedicated temporal binary multi-task model achieved 93.2\% binary detection accuracy with time-regression capability (MAE $= 1.33$h, R$^2 = 0.982$), while the unified temporal four-class multi-task model achieved 93.5\% derived binary accuracy together with 70.6\% four-class accuracy and MAE $= 1.34$h.
Bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals (1,000 iterations, well-level resampling; see STAR Methods) yield 93.5\% [88.4, 97.4] for the temporal model and 91.0\% [85.1, 96.6] for the single-frame baseline (Table~1).

The benefit of multi-task learning is most evident in the more challenging four-class severity task: temporal multi-task achieves 70.6\% four-class accuracy compared to 65.9\% for temporal classification-only (+4.7 pp), and 63.1\% vs.\ 54.7\% (+8.4 pp) for single-frame models (Table~1).
Empirically, the unified four-class multi-task model achieves 93.5\% derived binary accuracy, slightly higher than the dedicated binary multi-task model (93.2\%), suggesting that learning finer-grained severity features may provide complementary signal for binary discrimination.
For time regression within the unified four-class setting, temporal encoding improves MAE from 1.67h to 1.34h and R$^2$ from 0.971 to 0.981, confirming that temporal context benefits both binary robustness and secondary outputs.

\subsection*{Temporal encoding provides critical robustness at late time points}

The temporal advantage is most pronounced during late-stage infection (Figure~3).
Per-timepoint analysis in 3-hour bins reveals a clear pattern.
During the early phase (0--6 hours), both temporal and single-frame models perform comparably ($\sim$70\% binary accuracy), as CPE has not yet visibly manifested and the temporal model's earlier channels are partially clamped to the experiment boundary.
Between 7 and 36 hours, both models achieve high binary accuracy ($>$93\%), with temporal encoding providing marginal improvement as CPE actively develops.
During the late phase (40--48 hours), a dramatic divergence emerges: the single-frame model collapses to 75--80\% binary accuracy (dropping as low as 25\% for four-class), while the temporal model maintains 97--100\% binary accuracy.
At 43.5 hours, the temporal model achieves 100\% binary accuracy compared to 80.6\% for the single-frame model ($+$19.4 pp); at 46.5 hours, the advantage reaches $+$22.5 pp (97.5\% vs.\ 75.0\%).

This late-stage collapse of the single-frame model is attributable to high-confluency morphological ambiguity: extensively damaged infected monolayers share visual features with dense mock cultures when viewed in isolation.
The temporal model resolves this ambiguity by encoding the \emph{trajectory} of morphological change---a progressively deteriorating monolayer produces distinct color patterns in the pseudo-RGB representation compared to a stably confluent culture.

Complementing the late-stage robustness result, per-condition AUROC analysis (Figure~S3) confirms that the unified temporal model achieves AUROC\,$>$\,0.99 across all infection conditions from approximately 20 hours onward and sustains this through the full 46.5-hour imaging period.
Reliable detection is established within the first 20 hours for each condition, demonstrating that the model provides consistent performance throughout the entire infection timeline.
This combination of early detection and late-stage robustness is essential for practical applications such as antiviral screening, where reliable readouts are required across the full duration of an experiment.

\subsection*{Feature space analysis reveals improved class separation with temporal encoding}

To visualize the effect of temporal encoding on learned representations, we computed t-SNE embeddings of backbone features from both models (Figure~5).
The temporal model produces tighter, more clearly separated clusters for each infection condition, with distinct boundaries between infected classes and mock controls.
In contrast, the single-frame model shows substantial overlap between infection levels, particularly between MOI~5 and MOI~1 at late time points.

When features are colored by hours post-infection, the temporal model exhibits a smooth time gradient within each class cluster, indicating that the learned representation encodes both infection status and temporal progression.
This dual encoding is consistent with the multi-task training objective, which requires the shared backbone to support both classification and time regression.

\subsection*{Time regression achieves high accuracy with practical utility}

As a secondary output of the unified model, the regression head predicts time post-infection with MAE $= 1.34$ hours and R$^2 = 0.981$ for the temporal four-class multi-task model (Figure~6).
The temporal model consistently outperforms the single-frame four-class multi-task model (MAE $= 1.67$h, R$^2 = 0.971$) across the timeline, with the largest advantage during early hours: at 1.5 hours, the temporal model achieves MAE $= 0.36$h compared to 0.83h for single-frame (2$\times$ improvement).
This suggests that the temporal channels provide strong time-ordering cues even when classification confidence is low, as the frame-to-frame differences encode the rate of morphological change independent of absolute appearance.

\subsection*{Severity classification demonstrates dose-dependent feature learning}

As an additional output of the unified model, the temporal multi-task model achieves 70.6\% accuracy for four-class severity classification across MOI~5, MOI~1, MOI~0.1, and mock (Table~1), substantially outperforming the single-frame baseline (63.1\%).
Confusion matrix analysis (Figure~4) reveals that classification errors concentrate at biologically expected boundaries: MOI~5 is most commonly confused with MOI~1, and MOI~0.1---where only $\sim$10\% of cells are initially infected---is the hardest class throughout the timeline.
The dose-dependent classification difficulty (MOI~5 $>$ MOI~1 $>$ MOI~0.1) mirrors the expected biological gradient of CPE severity and supports the biological validity of the learned features.
While further improvement is needed for MOI-level classification to be practically actionable, these results provide proof-of-concept evidence that dose-dependent morphological features are learnable from label-free brightfield imaging.

\subsection*{Temporal offset ablation identifies optimal encoding span}

To investigate the effect of temporal span on classification performance, we trained models with three offset configurations (Table~2): short ($-$3, $-$1.5, 0 hours), standard ($-$6, $-$3, 0 hours), and long ($-$12, $-$6, 0 hours), in addition to the single-frame baseline ($0, 0, 0$ hours).

The single-frame baseline achieved 91.0\% binary accuracy (derived from four-class).
The short-span temporal model improved to 93.0\%, the standard span to 93.5\% (best binary and four-class accuracy), and the long span to 92.2\% (best regression MAE of 1.24h).
The standard ($-$6, $-$3, 0) configuration offers the best overall balance: it provides sufficient temporal context for late-stage discrimination while minimizing early-frame clamping artifacts.
The long-span model's superior regression performance is consistent with broader temporal context providing finer time-ordering cues, though the slight decrease in classification accuracy may reflect increased noise from spanning a wider morphological window.

\section*{DISCUSSION}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

\subsection*{Temporal encoding captures morphological trajectory and resolves late-stage ambiguity}

The most significant finding of this study is that encoding morphological trajectory---rather than static snapshots---dramatically improves infection detection robustness, particularly during late-stage infection.
The $+$22 percentage-point binary advantage at 40--48 hours represents the difference between a clinically useful system and one that generates false negatives precisely when monitoring is most critical.

The mechanism underlying this improvement is intuitive: in the pseudo-RGB representation, regions undergoing morphological change produce color shifts (e.g., a cell rounding over 6 hours appears as a red-to-blue gradient), while static regions appear gray.
A standard CNN trained on ImageNet has learned to exploit color patterns, and the temporal pseudo-RGB encoding repurposes this capability for temporal discrimination without any architectural modification.
Across the component comparison in Table~1, temporal encoding consistently improves both dedicated binary models and the unified four-class multi-task model, supporting the conclusion that learned features depend on morphological change over time rather than on static appearance alone.

In real-world infection monitoring scenarios---antiviral drug screening, biosafety assessment, longitudinal infection studies---reliable detection must be maintained throughout the full experiment duration, not just during the intermediate window where CPE is most visually obvious.
The single-frame model's catastrophic degradation after 40 hours arises from a specific biological confound: at advanced CPE stages, infected cells have largely detached or lysed, leaving a sparse debris field that, in brightfield microscopy, can resemble the dense confluent monolayer of healthy mock cultures.
The temporal model resolves this by capturing the trajectory leading to the current state: a well that has been progressively losing cells over hours looks fundamentally different in the pseudo-RGB representation from one that has maintained stable confluency, even if their current single frames are ambiguous.

Existing deep learning approaches to CPE detection---including Petkidis et al.\cite{ref-petkidis2024natcomm} (adenovirus and HSV, 88--100\% binary accuracy), Andriasyan et al.\cite{ref-andriasyan2021iscience} (adenovirus), and Chen et al.\cite{ref-chen2022biomedicines} (influenza, parainfluenza, enterovirus)---demonstrate strong binary performance but treat each frame independently, discarding the temporal trajectory inherent in time-lapse imaging.
AIRVIC\cite{ref-airvic2025} similarly operates on single frames.
None of these approaches have addressed late-stage morphological ambiguity, nor attempted dose-level severity classification from brightfield alone.
The present work addresses both gaps through a unified temporal formulation that is virus-agnostic and architecturally simple.

The simplicity of pseudo-RGB encoding is a deliberate design choice rather than a limitation.
Brightfield microscopy images are inherently grayscale (single-channel); pseudo-RGB encoding stacks three time-offset frames into a three-channel image that directly matches the input format expected by standard ImageNet-pretrained CNNs.
This repurposes the color-processing capabilities already learned from natural images for temporal discrimination, without requiring purpose-built temporal architectures such as 3D convolutional networks, recurrent models, or optical flow estimators.
Such architectures require substantially more data and training infrastructure and offer no interpretability advantage over the pseudo-RGB approach, which makes morphological change directly visible as color variation in the input.
The temporal offset ablation (Table~2) confirms that the approach is robust to offset choice, with the 3--12 hour span all outperforming the single-frame baseline.

\subsection*{Unified multi-task learning extends the model beyond binary detection}

The component ablation reveals that temporal encoding is the primary driver of binary detection improvement, while multi-task learning contributes most to the harder four-class severity task (+4.7 pp for temporal models, +8.4 pp for single-frame models).
This pattern is expected: binary discrimination between infected and uninfected is already well-served by temporal trajectory information, whereas distinguishing between dose levels (MOI~5 vs.\ MOI~1 vs.\ MOI~0.1) benefits from the additional supervisory signal provided by time regression, which encourages the shared backbone to learn temporally-ordered features that separate overlapping severity levels.
Empirically, the four-class multi-task model's derived binary accuracy (93.5\%) slightly exceeds the dedicated binary multi-task model (93.2\%), consistent with the view that learning fine-grained severity features provides complementary signal for coarser binary discrimination.\cite{ref-caruana1997mtl}

Our final model is intentionally unified: it is trained as a temporal four-class multi-task model, but its primary practical value remains robust binary infected-versus-mock detection. This formulation allows the same forward pass to preserve the binary headline result while also exposing severity and time-since-infection estimates when those outputs are useful.
The 70.6\% four-class accuracy across four MOI levels---from brightfield microscopy alone---provides proof-of-concept evidence that dose-dependent morphological features are learnable from label-free imaging, a capability not previously demonstrated in this context.
The dose-dependent difficulty gradient (MOI~5 easiest, MOI~0.1 hardest) reflects genuine biology: at MOI~0.1, only ${\sim}$10\% of cells are initially infected, and the resulting heterogeneous, delayed CPE is a fundamental challenge for well-level classification.

From a practical perspective, the multi-task formulation enables a unified workflow: a single forward pass produces (1) binary infection status, (2) four-class severity estimate, and (3) continuous time-since-infection prediction, supporting diverse downstream analysis needs without maintaining separate models.

\subsection*{Limitations of the study}

Several limitations should be acknowledged.
First, while validated across three independent biological replicates, all experiments used the same HBMVEC passage range and VEEV TC-83 vaccine strain. Generalization to virulent VEEV strains, other alphaviruses, or different cell types requires further investigation.
Second, the four-class accuracy of 70.6\%, while substantially above chance (25\%) and representing a $+$7.5 pp improvement over the single-frame baseline, leaves room for improvement. The primary confusions between adjacent MOI levels (5 vs.\ 1, 1 vs.\ 0.1) may reflect genuine biological overlap in CPE phenotypes.
Third, temporal encoding requires sequential imaging data and introduces a latency of 6 hours before full three-channel input is available; for the initial frames, clamped padding reduces effective temporal information.
Fourth, the fixed temporal offsets ($-$6, $-$3, 0h) may not be optimal across all virus--cell systems; adaptive offset selection could be explored in future work.
Finally, infection labels are assigned at the well level based on experimental conditions; individual cells within infected wells may remain uninfected, particularly at low MOI, and orthogonal single-cell validation (e.g., immunofluorescence) was not performed.

\subsection*{Experimental model and study participant details}

\subsubsection*{Cell lines}

Primary human brain microvascular endothelial cells (HBMVEC; Cell Systems, ACBRI~376; lot~376.07.05.01.2F) were obtained commercially and cultured in EGM-2 Endothelial Cell Growth Medium-2 BulletKit (Lonza, Cat\# CC-3162) at 37$^{\circ}$C and 5\% CO$_2$.
Per the vendor certificate of analysis, the cell preparation passed independent sterility testing (bacterial, fungal, and mycoplasma) and was validated as endothelial by immunofluorescence for CD31 ($>$98\% positive), von~Willebrand Factor / Factor~VIII ($>$95\% positive), and cytoplasmic uptake of Di-I-Ac-LDL ($>$95\% positive); donor was male and pediatric, and reported viability at receipt was 92\%.
Cells were used within passages~5--8 for the imaging experiments analyzed in this study.
No sex-based comparisons were performed in this study, which used a single commercially obtained primary HBMVEC preparation as an in vitro BBB model.

\subsubsection*{Virus strains}

The attenuated Venezuelan equine encephalitis virus (VEEV) vaccine strain TC-83 was used for all infection experiments.
Virus inoculum was prepared from P1 laboratory stock generated from an infectious cDNA clone originally provided by I.~Frolov\cite{ref-kinney1993tc83} and maintained under approved institutional biosafety procedures.
All work involving VEEV TC-83 was conducted under BSL-2 containment with institutional biosafety approval.

\subsection*{Method details}

\subsubsection*{Viral infection protocol}

Cells were seeded at a density of 45,000 cells per well in 12-well tissue-culture plates (CELLSTAR, Greiner Bio-One, Cat\# 665180) to obtain a confluent monolayer suitable for morphological assessment.
VEEV TC-83 was used for infection at three multiplicities of infection: MOI~5, MOI~1, and MOI~0.1. These MOI levels were selected to span a range of CPE kinetics from rapid synchronous infection (MOI~5) to delayed heterogeneous responses (MOI~0.1).
A mock-infected control condition was treated with PBS in parallel.
After viral adsorption, cells were washed with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) and fresh medium was added (defined as experiment start, $t=0$).

\subsubsection*{Experimental design and biological replicates}

Three independent biological replicates were performed on separate days and are referred to here as Run~1, Run~2, and Run~3 for presentation clarity.
At the dataset level, the acquisition hierarchy is run $\rightarrow$ well $\rightarrow$ position $\rightarrow$ timepoint $\rightarrow$ temporal pseudo-RGB sample (Figure~S6).
Each biological replicate used a separate 12-well plate with four conditions (MOI~5, MOI~1, MOI~0.1, and Mock) and three technical replicate wells per condition (rows A, B, and C).
Each well was imaged at 36 acquisition positions in a 6$\times$6 grid pattern over 96 time points collected every 30 minutes over 48 hours.
This design yields a hierarchical dataset comprising 3 biological runs, 12 wells per run, 36 acquisition positions per well, and 96 frames per position, for a total of 124,416 frame-level samples.

\subsubsection*{High-frequency live-cell imaging}

Imaging was performed using the CELLCYTE X (CYTENA GmbH, Freiburg, Germany) live-cell imaging system located inside a standard incubator.
Images were acquired using a 10$\times$ objective in ``Enhanced Contour'' mode at a spatial resolution of 0.862~$\mu$m/pixel.
Each field of view was 2448$\times$2048 pixels ($\sim$0.8$\times$0.7~mm).
Images were acquired every 30 minutes over 48 hours, producing per-position time series stored as multi-frame TIFF stacks.

\subsubsection*{Temporal pseudo-RGB encoding}

To incorporate temporal context, each sample is represented as a three-channel image composed of frames at three time offsets relative to the current time point $t$:
\[
  I_{\mathrm{pseudo}}(t) = \bigl[\,f(t{-}6\text{h}),\;\; f(t{-}3\text{h}),\;\; f(t)\,\bigr],
\]
where $f(\tau)$ denotes the normalized grayscale frame at time $\tau$.
The red channel maps to the oldest frame ($t{-}6$h), green to an intermediate frame ($t{-}3$h), and blue to the current frame ($t$).
Regions undergoing morphological change appear as color shifts, while static regions appear gray.
This allows a standard RGB-pretrained CNN to leverage its learned color-processing features for temporal discrimination without architectural modification.

For frames near the experiment start where offsets reach before $t=0$, the earliest available frame is used (clamp padding): $f(\tau) = f(\max(0, \tau))$.
As a baseline, the single-frame model replicates the same grayscale frame across all three channels.

\subsubsection*{Dataset construction and preprocessing}

Data were organized into four conditions based on MOI level.
Each frame was assigned a four-class label based on experimental condition ($y_{\mathrm{cls}} \in \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$ for MOI~5, MOI~1, MOI~0.1, and Mock).
Binary labels were derived post-hoc by collapsing classes 0--2 into ``Infected.''
A deterministic 5\%-per-side center crop was applied to mitigate potential overlap between adjacent acquisition positions ($\sim$5\%), followed by resizing to 512$\times$512 pixels.
Data augmentation (random horizontal/vertical flips and 90$^{\circ}$/180$^{\circ}$/270$^{\circ}$ rotations) was applied to training data only.

\subsubsection*{Cross-well row-split validation}

To assess cross-well generalization, we adopted a row-split validation protocol: rows A and B (2 of 3 technical replicate wells per condition within each biological run) were used for training, and row C was reserved for testing.
This ensures that training and test data come from physically separate wells.
To address condition-specific confounds, a well-swap protocol (v2 split) was applied: in Run~2, the MOI~1 well from row~C was exchanged with row~A; in Run~3, the MOI~5 well from row~C was exchanged with row~A.
These swaps preserve train/test MOI balance while mitigating anomalous well-specific effects (unusually low cell density in specific wells) identified during quality-control review before final model training.
The held-out test partition therefore consists of 12 wells across the three biological runs and contains 41,472 frame-level samples.

\subsubsection*{Multi-task architecture and training}

We used a ResNet50\cite{ref-he2016resnet} backbone initialized with ImageNet weights as the shared feature encoder, feeding into two task-specific heads:
(1) a classification head with a hidden layer (256 units, ReLU, dropout $p=0.2$) producing class logits, and
(2) a regression head with identical hidden structure outputting a scalar time prediction.
The loss function is:
\[
  L = \lambda_{\mathrm{cls}} \cdot L_{\mathrm{CE}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{reg}} \cdot L_{\mathrm{SL1}},
\]
where $L_{\mathrm{CE}}$ is cross-entropy loss and $L_{\mathrm{SL1}}$ is Smooth L1 (Huber) loss, with $\lambda_{\mathrm{cls}} = \lambda_{\mathrm{reg}} = 1.0$.
For single-task ablation experiments, the weight of the excluded task was set to zero.

Models were trained end-to-end for a fixed 30 epochs using AdamW (learning rate $1 \times 10^{-4}$, weight decay $1 \times 10^{-4}$) with cosine annealing ($T_{\max} = 30$, $\eta_{\min} = 10^{-6}$), automatic mixed precision, gradient norm clipping (max~1.0), and batch size 300.
Evaluation was performed every 5 epochs on the held-out test set for progress monitoring only; no early stopping or test-set-driven model selection was performed, and all headline results are reported from the fixed final epoch.

\subsection*{Quantification and statistical analysis}

Classification performance was evaluated using accuracy, macro-averaged F1-score, precision, and recall, computed across the full test set ($n = 41{,}472$ frame-level samples from held-out wells).
For the unified four-class model, binary metrics were derived by remapping four-class predictions (classes 0--2 $\to$ Infected, class 3 $\to$ Mock).
Regression was evaluated using mean absolute error (MAE), root mean square error (RMSE), and coefficient of determination (R$^2$).
All metrics were additionally computed in 3-hour time bins across the 0--48h timeline for temporal analysis.
To quantify uncertainty in reported metrics, bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals were computed from the per-sample prediction files (epoch~30) using well-level resampling.
Specifically, the 12 held-out test wells were resampled with replacement 1,000 times; for each resample, all frame-level predictions from the selected wells were aggregated and the metric of interest was recomputed.
The 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the resulting bootstrap distribution define the 95\% CI.
Well-level resampling was chosen because individual wells represent independent biological units; frame-level resampling would underestimate uncertainty by treating temporally correlated frames as independent observations.
All experiments were run with a single random seed; variability across biological replicates is therefore represented by the held-out well/run design rather than repeated training runs.
Software: Python 3.10, PyTorch 2.0, torchvision (ResNet50 with ImageNet weights).
t-SNE visualizations\cite{ref-vandermaaten2008tsne} used scikit-learn\cite{ref-pedregosa2011sklearn} with perplexity~30 and 1,000 iterations on 2,048-dimensional backbone features.
